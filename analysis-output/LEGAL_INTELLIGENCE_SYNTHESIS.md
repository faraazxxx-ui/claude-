## 1. FMLA Retaliation — Strongest Claim

The temporal proximity between Dr. Rahman's FMLA leave (April 22 - May 19, 2024) for cardiac surgery (Brugada syndrome/ICD implantation) and subsequent adverse employment actions creates a strong prima facie case of retaliation.

**Key Precedents:**

**Pollard v. The New York Methodist Hospital**, 861 F.3d 374 (2d Cir. 2017) — The Second Circuit vacated summary judgment for the hospital, holding that a condition does not need to be life-threatening to qualify as a "serious health condition" under FMLA. The court stated: *"The FMLA was intended to protect employees from losing their jobs when they need to take time off for legitimate medical reasons."* Brugada syndrome with ICD implantation unquestionably meets this standard.

**Pagel v. TIN Inc.**, 695 F.3d 622 (7th Cir. 2012) — An employee with a heart condition was fired after taking intermittent FMLA leave. The Seventh Circuit reversed summary judgment for the employer, finding that suspicious timing between FMLA leave and termination constitutes evidence of retaliation. This is directly analogous to Dr. Rahman's timeline.

**Fayyaz v. UHS of Hartgrove, Inc.**, No. 1:20-CV-01570 (N.D. Ill. Mar. 31, 2022) — A UHS HR Director sued for FMLA retaliation. The court granted summary judgment to UHS based on documented performance issues and inconsistent testimony. This reveals UHS's likely defense playbook: attribute adverse actions to performance deficiencies rather than protected activities. Dr. Rahman's team must preemptively gather evidence to counter this strategy.

**Strategic Assessment:** Dr. Rahman's FMLA claim is strong because the timeline is compressed and the adverse actions are severe. The critical vulnerability is the criminal charge (assault, April 18, 2024), which UHS will argue constitutes a legitimate, non-retaliatory reason for termination. The defense must demonstrate that the criminal charge itself was part of the retaliatory scheme.

## 2. ADA Accommodation Failure — Brugada as Disability

Brugada syndrome with ICD implantation constitutes a disability under the ADA. UHS's failure to provide reasonable accommodations after Dr. Rahman's cardiac surgery creates a strong ADA claim.

**Key Precedents:**

**Thomas v. Liv Group Inc., et al.** — The court allowed a disability discrimination suit to proceed based on derogatory remarks about an employee's heart condition, finding such statements constituted a "highly probable inference of disability discrimination." The court also affirmed individual liability for supervisors.

**David v. Postal Presort, Inc.** — A jury found the employer violated the ADA by refusing a brief six-day extension of medical leave for an employee with a heart condition. The employer's "undue hardship" defense was rejected and upheld on appeal. This precedent strengthens Dr. Rahman's position that even minor accommodation requests must be granted.

**EEOC v. Physicians' Specialty Hospital**, Civil Action No. 5:15-cv-05237-TLB — The EEOC sued a hospital for failing to accommodate a nurse with a seizure disorder. The hospital's failure to consider a transfer or leave of absence led to the lawsuit. This is directly analogous to Dr. Rahman's circumstances as a medical resident.

**Strategic Assessment:** The ADA claim is strengthened by the medical documentation of Brugada syndrome and ICD implantation. Any fitness-for-duty evaluation demands must be challenged under the EEOC's "job-related and consistent with business necessity" standard. The key is demonstrating that UHS had no objective evidence that Dr. Rahman's managed cardiac condition impaired his ability to function.

## 3. NYHRL Individual Liability — Dr. Ali and Doug

New York Human Rights Law Executive Law Section 296(6) provides for individual liability against supervisors who aid and abet discrimination or retaliation. This is a significant departure from federal law and creates personal exposure for Dr. Ali and Doug.

**Key Precedents:**

**Magnotti v. Crossroads Healthcare Mgmt. LLC**, 14-cv-6679 (E.D.N.Y. 2016) — The court found it plausible that a direct supervisor who knew about an employee's condition and was involved in employment decisions could be held individually liable under NYHRL aiding-and-abetting provisions.

**Lewis v. Triborough Bridge & Tunnel Authority**, 1999 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 17850 (S.D.N.Y. 1999) — A supervisor's failure to investigate and remedy complaints of harassment can lead to individual liability as an aider and abettor.

**Strategic Assessment:** This is a high-leverage claim because it creates personal financial exposure for the individual actors, which often accelerates settlement negotiations. The key is documenting that Dr. Ali and Doug were aware of the discriminatory conduct and failed to act, or actively participated in it.

## 4. UHS Corporate Liability and Pattern of Misconduct

UHS has a documented history of 53 violations since 2000, with penalties totaling over $197 million. Seventeen are employment-related offenses costing over $25 million. The 2020 False Claims Act settlement of $122 million demonstrates a corporate culture where profits are prioritized over compliance.

**Key Precedents:**

**United States v. Universal Health Services, Inc.**, No. 1:07CV00054 (W.D. Va.) — The court dismissed claims against the parent company, finding that mere ownership is insufficient to pierce the corporate veil. Specific facts showing direct involvement in wrongful conduct are required.

**U.S. ex rel. Hockett v. Columbia/HCA Healthcare Corp.**, 498 F. Supp. 2d 25 (D.D.C. 2007) — Established a two-prong test for veil-piercing: (1) unity of interest and ownership, and (2) inequitable result if acts are treated as subsidiary's alone. Plaintiffs survived summary judgment, demonstrating that piercing is possible in healthcare.

**Ivey v. Universal Health Services, Inc.**, No. 4:2023cv00355 (S.D. Ga. 2024) — Racial discrimination claim under Title VII. Court allowed amendment to name specific UHS subsidiary. This suggests targeting the specific Binghamton entity rather than the parent corporation.

**Strategic Assessment:** Direct claims against the Binghamton subsidiary are stronger than veil-piercing attempts against UHS parent. However, UHS's documented pattern of misconduct can be used to demonstrate corporate culture and support punitive damages arguments.

## 5. Criminal Defense — People v. Rahman (24-15519)

The concurrent criminal case is the most dangerous variable. UHS will use it as a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for all adverse actions.

**Key Precedents:**

**United States v. Scrushy**, 366 F. Supp. 2d 1134 (N.D. Ala. 2005) — The court suppressed deposition testimony where the government orchestrated a civil deposition as a "perjury trap." If UHS or its agents manipulated the criminal process to create a basis for termination, a due process challenge is viable.

**United States v. Stringer**, 535 F.3d 929 (9th Cir. 2008) — Sets a higher bar: mere coordination between civil and criminal proceedings is insufficient; affirmative misrepresentation or deceit must be proven.

**State v. Saavedra**, 222 N.J. 39 (2015) — If the criminal charge has a legitimate factual basis, courts may be reluctant to view it as retaliatory even with suspicious timing.

**Strategic Assessment:** A two-pronged strategy is necessary: (1) vigorously defend against the criminal charges on the merits, and (2) gather evidence of UHS's improper motive and coordination with law enforcement to demonstrate that the prosecution is a tool of retaliation. Discovery of communications between UHS and the prosecuting authorities is critical.

## 6. Immigration — J1 Visa / CIMT Risk (MOST TIME-SENSITIVE)

The immigration consequences are existentially consequential. A conviction for assault in New York may be classified as a Crime Involving Moral Turpitude (CIMT), which would be fatal to Dr. Rahman's immigration status.

**Key Precedents:**

**Matter of Solon**, 24 I&N Dec. 239 (BIA 2007) — Third-degree assault under New York Penal Law, requiring specific intent and physical injury, is a CIMT. This finding is directly relevant to any assault charge in New York.

**Matter of Fualaau**, 21 I&N Dec. 475 (BIA 1996) — Establishes framework for analyzing criminal offenses under immigration law.

**Strategic Assessment:** Criminal defense strategy must be closely coordinated with immigration counsel. Any plea deal must be scrutinized for immigration consequences. Even a deferred adjudication or expunged record can be considered a "conviction" by USCIS. The I-539 filing to extend J-1 status is critical and must be prepared immediately. Mandatory reporting to ECFMG within one business day of any criminal incident must be verified.
