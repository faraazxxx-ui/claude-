## Part I: The Geopolitical Landscape (Unbiased Assessment)

### 1.1 The Strait of Hormuz Crisis

The most consequential development for any Dubai-based shipping operation is the severe disruption of the Strait of Hormuz, which began on February 28, 2026. This is not a speculative risk scenario — it is the current operational reality. Iran has attacked at least 21 merchant vessels as of March 12, 2026 [6]. The crisis has been described as the "most severe Middle East supply chain disruption in modern history" [7].

The practical consequences for Minecore are immediate and severe. Maritime insurers have canceled most war-risk policies for the Persian Gulf [2] [3]. For the policies that remain available, premiums have surged by over 1000%, with the cost of hull war-risk premium for a single tanker voyage now estimated at approximately $7.5 million [8]. Major shipping lines, including Maersk, have suspended cargo shipments to and from the Arabian Gulf [9]. Container surcharges have reached $4,000 [10].

> "The Strait of Hormuz closure represents the most severe Middle East supply chain disruption in modern history."
> — Carraglobe Maritime Analysis, March 2026 [7]

This crisis compounds the already challenging Red Sea situation, where Houthi attacks on commercial shipping have been ongoing since early 2024 [11]. Dubai's maritime access is now constricted from both the west (Red Sea) and the east (Strait of Hormuz), creating a dual chokepoint crisis unprecedented in modern maritime trade.

### 1.2 Dubai's Eroding Safe-Haven Status

While DP World has stated that Jebel Ali port remains "fully operational," it concedes that inbound vessel traffic is reduced [12]. Other regional ports have suspended operations entirely [13]. The UAE is an active participant in the ongoing conflict, and has suffered both military and civilian casualties [14]. This direct involvement fundamentally undermines the narrative that Dubai exists in a protective bubble separate from regional instability.

The economic consequences are already materializing. Dubai's real estate and stock markets have shown declines [14]. Capital flight indicators are emerging, with reports of wealthy individuals exploring asset transfers to Switzerland and other neutral jurisdictions [4]. The US tariff regime, including a 50% tariff on UAE steel and aluminum exports, is compounding the economic pressure [15].

### 1.3 Why "Nothing Will Happen" Is the Most Dangerous Assumption

This section is written specifically to address the skepticism that the current situation will resolve without consequence. Three structural factors distinguish this crisis from past regional tensions:

**The Insurance Factor.** Even if every vessel in Minecore's fleet is physically safe in dock, the inability to secure war-risk insurance at a viable rate effectively halts operations. A shipping company cannot legally or financially operate uninsured vessels through a designated war zone. This is not a matter of courage or risk tolerance — it is a matter of regulatory and financial impossibility.

**The Chokepoint Reality.** Raw material exports are high-volume, low-margin operations. They cannot be pivoted to air freight. The Strait of Hormuz disruption is a direct hit to Minecore's core business model. The company's competitor, Emirates Global Aluminium (one of the UAE's largest industrial companies), has already been forced to reroute exports through the Port of Sohar in Oman [1]. If EGA — with vastly greater resources — has been forced to pivot, a smaller operator cannot afford to wait.

**The Regulatory Squeeze.** As an Indian national with assets in the UAE, Mr. Rahman faces a two-front regulatory challenge. India's Enforcement Directorate is actively investigating nationals who purchased property in Dubai using methods that may violate foreign exchange laws [4]. Simultaneously, a 2026 update to UAE civil law stipulates that assets of foreigners without UAE-compliant wills and legal heirs will be transferred to charitable organizations [4]. Inaction on estate planning is not merely imprudent — it is a direct path to asset loss.

## Part IV: Specific Risks for Indian Passport Holders in Dubai

### 4.1 Regulatory and Legal Risks

The Indian Enforcement Directorate (ED) is actively investigating Indian nationals who have used credit cards to purchase property in Dubai, citing potential violations of India's Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) [4]. This creates a direct legal risk for any Indian national with Dubai real estate acquired through non-standard financial channels.

A 2026 update to UAE civil law introduces a critical change: in the absence of a UAE-compliant will and legal heirs, the assets of a deceased foreigner will be transferred to charitable organizations [4]. For Indian nationals accustomed to Indian succession laws, this represents a fundamental shift that requires immediate legal action.

### 4.2 Repatriation and Mobility

The Indian Embassy in Abu Dhabi issued a travel advisory on March 5, 2026, confirming the temporary suspension of regular scheduled flights [5]. Over 52,000 Indian nationals were repatriated from the Gulf region between March 1 and March 7, 2026 [5]. While limited non-scheduled repatriation flights continue, the ability to move freely in and out of the region is significantly constrained.

### 4.3 Capital Controls

Reports indicate the UAE is implementing stricter regulations to prevent capital flight by foreign investors [4]. While specific details remain unconfirmed, this suggests a potential risk to the ability to liquidate and repatriate assets from the UAE in a timely manner. The window for orderly asset diversification may be closing.
